Course overview

Web Foundations

A hands-on introduction to real-world web development for senior high school students—offered during the school holidays.

Format — 5 days × 2 hours Total — 10 hours Style — Learn by doing (not lecture-only)

Goal of the course

The aim is to give students a realistic taste of how modern websites and web apps are built—using real tools that professionals use, in a structured, supportive classroom—not passive slides or toy “drag-and-drop” pages alone.

Students work toward understanding how the pieces fit together: the browser, the server, and stored data—so they leave with a clearer picture of what “full stack” means and whether they want to explore computing further.

One project from start to finish

The whole course centres on building and exploring a realistic social feed app (comments, replies, likes, sign-in, profiles—similar in spirit to familiar social products, but simplified for learning).

We begin with a working application. Each lesson, students either add a new feature or unpack an existing part (“reverse engineer” it) to learn a concept. That way, every change connects to something visible on screen.

How we teach (not by long lectures)

  • Step-by-step instructions first. Students follow clear directions and type or copy code with purpose. We encourage them to look things up, read error messages, and try again—building self-education and “teach yourself” habits, which matter far beyond this course.
  • See the effect, then the explanation. Early on, students may not fully grasp why a snippet works. They will see how a change in code shows up in the app. Deeper explanation follows once there is something concrete to point at.
  • Exercises are marked. Each exercise receives feedback so students know what they completed successfully and where to improve—supporting steady progress across the five days.

Daily schedule (overview)

Each two-hour session typically includes a short warm-up, instructor demonstration, hands-on work, and a brief wrap-up. Exact timings may shift slightly to suit the group.

Day Focus (parent-friendly summary)
Day 1 The big picture: how the web stack fits together—browser, network, server, and data. Students run the app and connect what they see on screen to files and requests.
Day 2 Pages, forms, and the browser—HTML structure, how forms send data, and light JavaScript to load and show content (the “client” side).
Day 3 The server—how a program listens for requests, routes URLs to code, and serves pages and files safely (using a modern runtime suited to the classroom project).
Day 4 Sign-in, sessions, and APIs—how accounts and permissions work at a high level, and how the browser and server agree on who is allowed to do what.
Day 5 Databases and going live—storing posts and users, simple queries, and what changes when an app is deployed to the internet (with sensible, age-appropriate discussion).

This outline matches the instructor lesson plan; vocabulary in class may be adjusted for clarity while keeping the same learning goals.

Practical expectations

Students should be comfortable using a computer keyboard, following written steps, and asking for help when stuck—curiosity and persistence are more important than prior programming experience.

About the instructor

Hi 👋 — I’m Jake. I’m a senior software engineer, passionate about fostering the next generation of young engineers. I studied Computer Science (Honours) at UNSW, and I’ve spent over ten years working in web development.

For me, this isn’t only about teaching technology—it’s about motivating young people to learn and experiment for themselves. In the age of AI, having a deep understanding of how technology works is essential; we can’t afford to blindly trust automated systems. Without that foundation, it’s easy to accept results without understanding why, or to find it harder to think independently. I created Web Foundations to help bridge that gap.
